Introduction¶

In this project, we hope to understand the reasons why a patient will either show up or fail to show up after booking an appointment with a doctor. We will be adopting the KaggleV2-May-2016 dataset to achieve this objective. This dataset collects information from 100k medical appointments in Brazil and is focused on the question of whether or not patients show up for their appointment.

Our data of interest has 14 features and the data we want to predict (No Show) is a categorical variable, which has two outome: Yes or No. Yes in this case means that the patient did not show up, while No means that the patient showed up. Liste below are the features in the dataset.

  1. PatientId
  2. AppointmentID
  3. Gender
  4. ScheduledDay
  5. AppointmentDay
  6. Age
  7. Neighbourhood
  8. Scholarship
  9. Hipertension
  10. Diabetes
  11. Alcoholism
  12. Handcap
  13. SMS_received
  14. No-show

We try to answer this question by looking at the relationship between the No-show feature in our dataset as against these variables.

  1. Gender: A patient can be male or female
  2. Scholarship: This implies that a patient can be enrolled or not enrolled in Brasilian welfare program
  3. Ailments: In this dataset, we treat features like Handicap, Hipertension, diabetes, Alcoholism as ailment. We then try to understand the impact of this feature on the outcome variable.
  4. Test Messages: If a patient received test messages or not.

Given these variables, we hope to answer the above question by asking these sets of question:

  1. Does a patient's gender determine if he or she will show up for an appointment?
  2. Does Scholarship increase the the rate at which people will show up for appointment?
  3. How does ailment such as Diabetes, Alcoholism, impact if a patient will show up for appointment or not?
  4. What role does information play in patients showing up for appointments?

Conclusions¶

Given the set of features in the dataset, we tried to understand how certain features will end up making a customer show up for an appointment. Thus, we asked the following questions:

  1. Does a patient's gender determine if he or she will show up for an appointment?
  2. Does Scholarship increase the rate at which people will show up for appointment
  3. How does ailment such as Diabetes, Alcoholism, impact if a patient will show up for appointment or not
  4. What role does information play in patients showing up for appointments

The approach in solving the problem

To answer these questions, we filtered the data in such a way that we had two set of variables for a particular question. For instance, in the aspect of gender, we filtered the data for rows with males and did the same for female. We then selected the No-Show column for both variable to reflect the number of males and females that showed up or did not show up for their appointment. This was repeated for other variables like Scholarship, ailments, SMS messages.

Although we followed the same approach in answering the questions highlighted above, we did something different with the third question. We filtered the data to reflect rows for which patients has hypertension or not. We then group by diabetes, alcoholism, handicap to know the number of customers that have hypertension and also fall under one of these categories. Similarly, we use the same approach to know the patients without hypertension that falls under one of these categories.

For each of the questions we tried to answer, we created a bar plot to show the number of people that showed up or failed to show up for a particular feature. Using SMS Messages for instance, we filtered the data to reflect the number of patients that received SMS and the number of people that did not receive SMS.

The idea was to understand if there was a correlation between the number of people that showed up for an appointment as against people that received messages. We then use bar plot to understand the number of people that received SMS and showed up for appointment and people who received SMS but failed to show up for appointment. We did the same thing for people that did not received SMS but show up for the appointment as against those that did not show up. The bar plot showed that in both cases (people that received or did not receive SMS), the number of people that showed up for appointment was high compared to people who did not show up for appointment.

According to our findings, there is no clear picture as per whether any of the variables we considered will cause patients to show up for an appointment or not. For instance, when we split gender into male and female, the number of patients that showed up for apointment in both cases is quite high. Similarly, when we split the data into people that received test messages or failed to receive test messages, the number of patients that show up for appointment is quite high in both categories.

Limitations

It would be important to know how far a patient's location is compared to the place of appointment. This might play a huge role in explaining why patient fail to show up for an appointment. However, the dataset we considered failed to take into consideration this factor.
